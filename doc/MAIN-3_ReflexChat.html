As a software architect, I look at systems not just as collections of code, but as living representations of a business. To understand **Screaming Architecture** and its relationship with **SOLID**, we have to shift our mindset from *technical frameworks* to *business capabilities*. Here is the breakdown of what it is, why it matters, and how it dictates your project structure. --- ### What is Screaming Architecture? Coined by Robert C. Martin ("Uncle Bob"), **Screaming Architecture** is the principle that the structure of your application—specifically its directory layout, file names, and module organization—should clearly communicate the **business purpose** of the application, not the framework or tools used to build it. If you look at the blueprints of a building, you immediately know if it’s a hospital, a library, or a house. You don't look at the blueprint and say, "Ah, this is a concrete-and-steel architecture." Similarly, when a new developer opens your repository, the root folders shouldn't scream *"Spring Boot!"*, *"React!"*, or *"Ruby on Rails!"* (e.g., `controllers/`, `models/`, `views/`). Instead, the project should scream *"I am a Healthcare System!"* (e.g., `PatientAdmissions/`, `Billing/`, `LabResults/`). **The Framework is a Detail.** The database is a detail. The web delivery mechanism is a detail. The architecture should hide these details and highlight the Use Cases. --- ### How Screaming Architecture Influences Project Structure When you combine Screaming Architecture with **SOLID principles**, you move away from **Technical Cohesion** (grouping files by what they *are*) to **Domain Cohesion** (grouping files by what they *do*). Here is how a traditional framework-driven structure compares to a Screaming Architecture structure: #### The "Framework-Screaming" Structure (Anti-pattern) ```text src/ ├── controllers/ <-- Technical grouping │ ├── OrderController │ └── UserController ├── models/ <-- Technical grouping │ ├── Order │ └── User └── repositories/ <-- Technical grouping ├── OrderRepository └── UserRepository ``` #### The "Domain-Screaming" Structure (SOLID-aligned) ```text src/ ├── Checkout/ <-- Screams "E-commerce!" │ ├── Domain/ <-- Enterprise business rules (Entities) │ ├── UseCases/ <-- Application business rules │ └── Infrastructure/ <-- DB, API, Framework adapters ├── Inventory/ <-- Screams "E-commerce!" │ ├── Domain/ │ ├── UseCases/ │ └── Infrastructure/ └── UserManagement/ <-- Screams "E-commerce!" ``` --- ### The Intersection of Screaming Architecture and SOLID When you structure your project to "scream" its domain, you are inherently applying SOLID principles at an architectural level. Here is how they influence the folder structure: #### 1. Single Responsibility Principle (SRP) *Architectural Translation: Group by reason to change.* In a framework-centric structure, if the business rules for "Checkout" change, you have to touch the `controllers`, `models`, and `services` folders. In a Screaming Architecture, the `Checkout/` module has one reason to change: the business rules of checking out. The UI and the Database are separated from the Use Cases, meaning a change to the database schema doesn't force a change in the core business logic. #### 2. Open/Closed Principle (OCP) *Architectural Translation: Core domain is closed to modification, open to extension via plugins.* Your project structure places the core business logic at the center. Frameworks, databases, and UIs are treated as "plugins" to the business rules. You can swap out a REST API for a GraphQL API by adding a new folder in the `Infrastructure/` layer without touching the `Domain/` or `UseCases/` folders. #### 3. Liskov Substitution Principle (LSP) *Architectural Translation: Infrastructure must fulfill domain contracts.* Your `Domain` folder will define interfaces (contracts). Your `Infrastructure` folder will contain the implementations. The architecture guarantees that the core application can substitute a `PostgresOrderRepository` with an `InMemoryOrderRepository` without the core domain knowing or caring. #### 4. Interface Segregation Principle (ISP) *Architectural Translation: Tailored boundaries.* Instead of having a massive `IUserRepository` in a shared folder, Screaming Architecture encourages specific interfaces inside specific use cases. The `Checkout` module might define an `ICheckoutCustomerReader`, while the `UserManagement` module defines an `IUserUpdater`. The project structure physically separates these concerns. #### 5. Dependency Inversion Principle (DIP) *Architectural Translation: The Dependency Rule.* This is the most critical principle for project structure. **Dependencies must point inward toward the domain.** * The `Domain/` folder has NO dependencies. It knows nothing about the outside world. * The `UseCases/` folder depends only on `Domain/`. * The `Infrastructure/` (Controllers, DB adapters) depends on `UseCases/` and `Domain/`. Because of DIP, your project structure forces developers to put database queries in the outer layers and inject them inward, ensuring the business logic remains pure and framework-agnostic. ### The Architect's Summary Screaming Architecture is the visual manifestation of **Clean Architecture** or **Hexagonal Architecture**. By applying SOLID principles at the directory level, you create a project structure where the business domain is the star of the show, and the technical implementation details are relegated to the edges of the system. This results in software that is highly testable, easily maintainable, and deeply aligned with the business it serves.
